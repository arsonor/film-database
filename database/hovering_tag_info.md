
## **THEMES**

**social** interpersonal relations within a community, class dynamics, or living conditions

**societal** how society functions as a system — institutions, norms, cultural shifts, systemic issues

**tragedy** a narrative driven by fate, inevitability, or a fatal flaw leading to downfall

**psychological** inner mental life of a character: their psyche, mental processes, perceptions, or psychological deterioration

**death** where mortality, grief, the meaning of death, or the process of dying is what the film is wrestling

**transformation** significant and radical change in a character's physical or mental state between beginning and end

**whimsical/zany** a playful, fanciful, logic-defying spirit. The film bends or ignores rules of reality in a lighthearted or imaginative way

## **ATMOSPHERES**

**family-friendly** watchable with children, parents, or any group without discomfort. This is about audience accessibility, not about families being depicted on screen (refer to Characters: 'relatives')

**feel good** a feel-good film can have adult themes; a family-friendly film isn't necessarily uplifting

**crazy/nutty** chaotic, exuberant, over-the-top energy. The film embraces excess and wild unpredictability in tone

**delicate/intimate** quiet, tender, emotionally close. The film handles its subject with softness and subtlety, often in a small-scale, personal setting

**contemplative/meditative** feeling of calm, stillness, and peaceful observation. Key indicators: static or slow camera, silence or sparse sound, ASMR-like quality, a sense of emotional distance or serenity

**oppressive** sustained, suffocating weight the viewer physically feels throughout the entire viewing experience

**meticulous** everything feels deliberate, ordered, controlled; exceptional precision and attention to detail in its storytelling, direction, or visual composition

**ethereal** light, floating, airy quality; feels weightless or suspended, as if existing slightly above reality

**hypnotic/immersive** trance-like or spellbound effect through cinematic techniques (rhythmic editing, repetition, slow motion, droning or pulsating sound design, visual intensity, sustained tension that locks focus)

**psychedelic** vivid, hallucinatory, sensory-overload visual or audio experience

**steamy** strong erotic charge or sexual tension pervading the atmosphere

**sordid** moral degradation, sleaze, depravity, or a world of seedy, ugly reality

**cityscape** the urban environment as a defining visual and narrative presence

**pastoral** the countryside, rural life, or natural landscape as a defining atmospheric presence

**gritty/realistic** feels like raw and real life, grounded in harsh or unglamorous reality without cinematic polish

**epic** grand in scale, scope, or ambition with large canvases, sweeping narratives, a sense of historical or emotional magnitude


## **CHARACTERS**

**ensemble cast** multiple distinct characters in separate storylines that eventually intersect or parallel each other

**adult/child** central relationship or dynamic is between an adult and a child

**female** places female experience, perspective, or identity at its core

**ordinary** ordinariness of a character's life is a deliberate artistic choice, not whenever ordinary people appear on screen but draws meaning from the mundane

**simpleton/fool** naivety, simplicity, or apparent stupidity is a defining trait, often used for comic effect or as an unexpected lens on the world

**disabled** significant physical or cognitive disability that is explicitly depicted and relevant to the narrative

**outcast/misfit** social rejection or a profound inability to belong

**double** duplicated, mirrored, or has a doppelgänger; can be literal (twins, clones) or metaphorical (split personality, alter ego)

**cross-dressing** disguises as another character/gender, whether for comic, dramatic, or identity-related purposes

**unreliable narrator** the story is told through a perspective that the audience eventually discovers is distorted, biased, or deliberately deceptive

**antihero** lack conventional heroic qualities but the audience follows and often roots for them despite their flaws, selfishness, or moral ambiguity

**vigilante** takes justice into their own hands outside the law

**vehicle** as a character or as a central narrative element


## **MOTIVATIONS**

**love** deep emotional attachment in the broadest sense: romantic, parental, fraternal, platonic

**emancipation** process of freeing from control, constraint, or dependence; also covers apprenticeship and coming-of-age growth when the core arc is about gaining autonomy

**communication** act of communicating (or failing to communicate), misunderstandings, language barriers, the struggle to connect or express oneself

**honor/duty** acts out of obligation, moral code, or loyalty to a principle larger than self-interest

**obsession** a pathological, consuming fixation that distorts a character's behavior and judgment, takes over and damages the character or those around them

**manipulation** deliberately controls, deceives, or psychologically exploits others to achieve their goals

**sacrifice** deliberately gives up something vital (safety, love, life) for another person or cause

**greed/ambition** pursuit of wealth, status, or power as a personal drive

**perversion** sexual desire that transgresses social norms or moral boundaries, taken to a dark or disturbing extreme

**fight** significant action/combat scenes, not just metaphorical struggles

**world-saving** the stakes are global or existential: the characters are trying to prevent large-scale destruction or save humanity/the world.


## **MESSAGES**

**humanist** human compassion, dignity, or the value of every individual life

**anti establishment** challenges or criticizes institutional power, authority, or the status quo

**traditionalist/way of life** particular cultural tradition, lifestyle, or set of values, often rural, ancestral, or community-based

**revisionist/alternate history** deliberately reinterprets, subverts, or reimagines historical events or genre conventions

**surreal** deliberate, often jarring juxtapositions of impossible or irrational elements

**dreamlike** floating, oneiric quality that feels dream-like in texture, regardless of whether actual dreams appear in the plot (refer to 'dream' in Themes)

**symbolic** images, objects, characters, or situations as symbols carrying meaning beyond their literal narrative function, the viewer is invited to read beneath the surface

**poetic** lyrical quality in its pacing, imagery, or language that prioritizes beauty, rhythm, and emotional resonance over narrative efficiency


## **CINEMA TYPES**

**aesthetics** strong, distinctive visual and auditory identity, not simply "a beautiful film" — the "look" is inseparable from the film's meaning

**expressionism** early 20th-century movement originating in German cinema, using extreme visual distortion to externalize psychological states and inner turmoil

**realism** movement seeking to depict everyday social life without artifice, focusing on working classes, fatalism, and unembellished truth

**neo-realism** Italian post-war movement that broke radically with studio filmmaking: real locations, non-professional actors, natural light, and stories about ordinary people struggling in the ruins of war and poverty

**noir** American film movement defined by cynical narratives, moral ambiguity, femmes fatales, hard-boiled dialogue, and a distinctive visual style of high-contrast lighting, deep shadows, and urban night settings

**neo-noir** revival and reinterpretation of noir themes and aesthetics in contemporary settings

**hollywood golden age** era of the major studio system, when a handful of studios controlled production, distribution, and exhibition. Defined by star-driven glamour, strict genre conventions, the Production Code (moral censorship), lavish production values, and a polished, studio-controlled aesthetic

**new wave** primarily refers to the French Nouvelle Vague, a revolutionary movement led by young critics-turned-filmmakers from Cahiers du Cinéma. Characterized by location shooting, jump cuts, breaking the fourth wall, improvised dialogue, literary and cinematic references, and a rejection of traditional "quality" filmmaking. Also applies broadly to similar national movements (Czech, Japanese, British New Wave)

**new hollywood** era when a generation of film-school-educated directors broke from studio conventions to make auteur-driven, often darker and more politically engaged films, enabled by the collapse of the Production Code and the new MPAA rating system. Characterized by anti-establishment themes, morally ambiguous protagonists, location shooting, and a willingness to challenge audiences

**blockbuster** large-scale mainstream commercial entertainment, with high production budgets, wide theatrical release, and broad audience appeal as primary goals 

**art house** artistic expression rather than mass commercial appeal, typically with limited theatrical release, festival circulation, or independent distribution

**B** a low-budget commercial film, historically the second feature in a double bill. Fast-paced genre entertainment (horror, sci-fi, westerns) with modest production values and lesser-known casts. Not the same as "bad film" or "indie film"

**generational** deeply representative of a specific generation's identity, lifestyle, struggles, or cultural moment

**popular culture** explicitly references, quotes, or is steeped in elements of shared popular culture (other movies, music, brands, TV, memes). The film speaks through pop culture. Not used simply because the film itself became popular

**multi-sequence** the narrative is structured in clearly distinct segments: multiple storylines, chapters, anthology-like episodes, or radically different points of view

**sequence-shot** features notable use of long, unbroken takes as a defining stylistic choice. Not for films that simply have one or two long shots

**slow cinema** a formal filmmaking movement characterized by extremely long takes, minimal dialogue, static camera, and deliberate pacing as artistic statement

**dogma** following (or inspired by) the Dogme 95 manifesto: natural lighting, handheld camera, no special effects, diegetic sound only

**dialogs** writing quality, wit, or density of dialogue is a defining characteristic. The words themselves are a major reason to watch the film

**slang dialogs** the language is rooted in street speech, regional vernacular, argot, or subcultural jargon. The way characters speak is authentically tied to a particular milieu and is central to the film's texture

**fait divers/true incident** a specific real-life incident, case, or news story — typically a crime, scandal, trial, accident, or unusual event

**costume drama** historical setting, costumes, and production design are central to the experience

**swashbuckler** adventure films centered on sword-fighting heroes in historical or romanticized settings, typically involving dashing protagonists, daring rescues, and flamboyant action

**wu xia pian** Chinese martial arts genre rooted in the literary tradition of wuxia (literally "martial heroes")

**blaxploitation** 1970s American film movement featuring predominantly Black casts in genre films (action, crime, horror) made primarily for Black urban audiences. Also applies to later films that consciously reference or revive the aesthetic

**giallo** Italian thriller/horror genre characterized by elaborate murder sequences, stylized visuals, mystery-driven plots, and distinctive scores

**slasher** horror subgenre centered on a killer (often masked or anonymous) systematically murdering victims in graphic and suspenseful sequences

**docufiction** dramatizing real events or situations with a documentary feel, or uses documentary format applied to entirely fictional subjects
